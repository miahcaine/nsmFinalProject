HISTORY OF CRIME AND STOP AND FRISK

Have Stop and Frisks Been Effective?

Over 2003-2016, stop and frisks were most often conducted for criminal possession of a weapon, grand larceny, robbery, burglary, and criminal trespass. However, whether the high frequency correlated with a corresponding decrease in respective crime rates is still being discussed today. Interact with the slider below to see how rates for these top five crimes have changed, if at all, over the time period.

Where are these stops occurring? And how frequently?

There were over 5 million stops between 2003 and 2016, peeking in 2011. After 2011 there is a drastic decrease in the number of stops after the practice was ruled unconstitutional in 2012. Hover over the timeline to see the number of stops per year. Click on a precinct or use the select box below to show that precinct's timeline of stops and understand the differences in the amount of stops conducted between precincts.

In 2010, Officer Adrian Schoolcraft released secretly taped recordings of meetings and interactions between officers in the 81st Precinct. The 81st Precinct is located in Bedford-Stuyvesant, a Brooklyn neighborhood that is historically Black. Here are some quotes that stand out.



"[Your] not working in Midtown Manhattan where people are walking around smiling and happy. You’re working in Bed-Stay where everyone’s probably got a warrant.

- Lieutenant Jean Delafuente, November 1, 2008


Tonight is zero tolerance. It’s New Years Eve all over again. Everybody goes. I don’t care . . . . They’re throwing dice? They all go, promote gambling. I don’t care. Let the DA discuss what they’re going to do tomorrow . . . . They got [bandanas] on and they’re running like nuts down the block, chasing people? Grab them. Fuck it. You’re preventing a robbery . . . . You know that and I know that.

- Deputy Inspector Steven Mauriello, October 31, 2008


All right, I went out there [to Howard and Chauncey] yesterday and . . . we’ve got the old man out there with the grey hairs. A loud mouth. He thinks since he’s 55 years old he’s not going to get locked up. Well, guess what? I don’t tolerate shit out there. He went in and two of his pals went in. All right? So we’ve got to keep the corner clear. . . . Because if you get too big of a crowd there, you know, . . . they’re going to think that they own the block. We own the block. They don’t own the block, all right? They might live there but we own the block. All right? We own the streets here. You tell them what to do.

- Lieutenant Jean Delafuente, November 8, 2008


“If they’re on a corner, make them move. They don’t want to move, you lock them up. Done deal. You can always articulate later.”

- Sergeant Raymond Stukes, November 23, 2008

Who was targeted?

Though the 'intention' of stop and frisk was to find criminals, stop and frisk seemed to target particular demographics while leaving other demographics relatively unaffected. Of the over 5 million stops between 2003 and 2016, let these 1000 dots be representative of all of the stops. Click the buttons on the right to see how these 1000 dots are distributed based on a particular statistic.

Listen to the words of Kasiem Walters of Flatbush Brooklyn, who by the time he was a high school senior, had been stopped by police 7 times.



"The thing that changed for me first is the way I looked at police. I couldn't look at them the same. Um, I remember, I mean it was bad to a point where I got robbed I didn't call the police. They scare you you know, so they instill this fear in you and then it forces you to have this mindset that you are a criminal."

Listen to the words of Adhyl Polanco, a police officer who grew up in Washington Heights, Manhattan. He, himself has been stopped by police even after he joined the force.



"We would stop a person in the street at the corner because the sergeant said stop. Why? You don’t ask. You stop him, you frisk him, if it’s possible you search him. And these kids, sometimes they’re just walking home from school, they’re just walking to the store, they’re not doing absolutely anything."
