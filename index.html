What is Stop, Question, and Frisk?


Stop, Question, and Frisk, also known by Stop and Frisk, is the police practice of stopping those suspected of having committed, are currently committing, or are about to commit a crime. Several things can happen for the duration of a stop like some questioning, a light frisk on the outside of the body, a full on search of the person and their belongings, and an ultimate arrest. The predecessor to stop and frisk was the 1968 Supreme Court Case Terry v. Ohio, which ruled that police officers do not need probable cause, but only reasonable suspicion to stop and question individuals. However, the introduction of CompStat in the 1990s which was used to essentially “predict” where crime was most likely to occur changed the typically 'terry' stop. Whereas before, police were required to let go people they had stopped with no subsequent search if further questioning did not raise levels of suspicion, the new norm was to stop, question, frisk, and search potential criminals under a police officers’ discretion.

Have Stop and Frisks Been Effective?

Over 2003-2016, stop and frisks were most often conducted for criminal possession of a weapon, grand larceny, robbery, burglary, and criminal trespass. However, whether the high frequency correlated with a corresponding decrease in respective crime rates is still being discussed today. Bloomberg Takes Stop and Frisk to Unprecedented Levels


The 1999 killing of 23-year-old unarmed Black man, Amadou Diallo, raised questions and concerns regarding stop and frisk in relation to racial profiling. This resulted in the requirement that police officers record information about their stops including things like race, gender, and age. Though Michael Bloomberg did not create stop and frisk, when he became the mayor of New York City in 2002, stop and frisk numbers soared. As mayor, he became a major advocate of stop and frisk, dismissed concerns regarding racial profiling, and believed that the practices led to decreased crime rates. His claims regarding crime remain disputed to this day, as other sources have noted that crime decreased before stop and frisk was implemented, and after it was cut back.

Stop and Frisk: The Aftermath


Stop and Frisk peaked in 2011, closing in on almost 700,000 stops that year alone. In 2013, a federal judge ruled in the case of Floyd v. City of New York that stop and frisk was unconstitutional and that it violated the 4th amendment, the 14th amendment, and Section VI of the Civil Rights Act. Bloomberg left office and Bill de Blasio assumed his position as mayor 2014. Though during his time in office, Stop and Frisk numbers dramatically plummeted, many believe that racial disparities in New York City policing remain. Though the height of stop and frisk is in the past, it leaves a legacy that will be very hard to break from. Its effect on crime is at best debatable, and at its worst ineffective. What is undebatable, however, is its effect on young men of color. To quote a New York Times Article:


“We now know that students heavily exposed to stop-and-frisk were more likely to struggle in school, that young men were more likely to experience symptoms of anxiety and depression, that this exposure fostered cynicism in policing and government writ large, and that it made residents more likely to retreat from civic life.”
